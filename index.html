02 Building and prototyping
⚡

Building and
prototyping with AI

We've explored a few tools for prototyping and interaction exploration — each taught us something different about where AI fits in the build process.

Figma Make

Prototyping for UTs

Useful for exploring layout ideas and generating realistic prototypes for UTs in a shorter time than traditional prototyping tools. However, this takes time to set up and requires a lot of manual tweaking and prompting to get the prototype to the desired state.

Google Stitch

AI-Generated UI Prototypes

Google Stitch lets you generate functional UI screens from text prompts and sketches. Useful for rapidly exploring visual directions and getting to a tangible design artefact without starting from scratch. Still being explored — early results suggest strong potential for early-stage ideation and stakeholder alignment.

03 Research & Synthesis
🔬

AI as a research partner

AI has the potential to handle volume — surfacing patterns across large datasets of interview notes and transcripts. But the insight still belongs to us.

Research Planning

Drafting user research plans

Used AI to scaffold research plans from a brief — structuring goals, methods, and discussion guides. Saved time on structure. Human judgment was still essential for method selection and question design.

Dovetail, NotebookLLM

Synthesising interview insights

Uploaded transcripts and synthesis notes and asked AI to surface recurring themes. Useful for getting an orientation across a large dataset — not as a replacement for close reading and researcher interpretation.

⚠️ Fact-checking always sits with us. AI-synthesised insights are a starting point — always verify against primary sources before drawing conclusions.
04 Writing & Working with AI
✍️

Writing &
Working with AI

From first-draft collaborator to shared vocabulary — here's what we learned about getting useful output, consistently.

AI-Assisted Writing

Drafts, copy, and communication

We used AI to draft feature announcements, explore UX microcopy variations, and write first cuts of stakeholder update emails. The output is consistently fast and structurally sound. The voice, nuance, and judgment — that's still an edit pass away. Best results came when we gave it specific context, tone guidelines, and clear constraints.

Lessons Learned

Precise naming and shared language matter

Vague prompts produce vague outputs. When our team aligned on shared language — consistent component names, user types, workflow stages — AI outputs became far more relevant and reusable across sessions. The effort we put into naming things clearly for ourselves translated directly into better AI collaboration.

🤝 Treat AI like a new collaborator joining your team: it needs context, constraints, and a clear brief. The more specific you are, the more useful it becomes.
AI writes fast. Good writing still takes time — and garbage in, garbage out.
05 Daily Workflow
⚙️

AI in Your
Daily Workflow

Not every AI use case needs to be a big experiment. Some of the most useful moments happen quietly, day-to-day.

Cowork

Turning plans into visual outlines

Cowork takes a plan or task list and turns it into a structured visual outline — great for kicking off project planning sessions or aligning a team quickly on scope before diving into detailed work. It bridges the gap between a rough idea and a structured brief.
